Understanding the problem

More than half of adults in Canada take at least 1 prescription medication each month. Following instructions and using medications exactly as prescribed helps people stay as healthy as possible with the least risk of harm. While medication routines are simple on paper, in reality, they often clash with busy schedules, classes, work and other responsibilities.

There are simply too many steps, too many reminders and too much uncertainty.

At the same time, healthcare is increasingly self-managed between appointments. People are expected to track dosages, refill prescriptions and show up to their appointments prepared. Most rely on a patchwork of different systems including alarms, notes, pill bottles and calendars. In the end, the cognitive load of trying to remember everything is frustrating.

When tracking feels tedious, it gets dropped.

There is a gap in the market for a new approach.

Research and Analysis

In order to understand the pain points of adults regularly taking one or more prescription medications, a survey was conducted. This survey provides insights into the habits and struggles of potential users. As the user success of the app is dependent on adherence, the feedback received was vital to assess demand and test the idea prior to development.

The goal behind the survey is to understand how often people miss doses, what makes adherence hard and whether a medication and appointment reminder app would be useful.

The survey data highlighted multiple pain points concerning the reasons for why people struggle with adherence to their medications. These results will be referenced during the development in order to create a product that aligns with the market demand.

Personas

The obtained survey data can be used to create several profiles of possible users. These personas highlight user expectations, concerns and other information. The following personas provide a helpful representation of the target market which will allow for a more personalized product and user experience.

User Journey

A user journey was created using the persona of Richard. The goal behind this is to identify pain points and guide the creation of an app that will address them directly.

This journey highlights Richard’s struggle to balance his professional life and manage his health. From the journey, it’s clear that he struggles with follow-through and overwhelm. He would benefit from a solution that minimizes his cognitive load while being portable and easily referenced while at work or during his commute.

Visual Design

Color Palette

The color palette was chosen based on several factors. Firstly, note the use of Black and White. These are intentionally not absolute in value to give them a more organic look and feel. The color blue was chosen because it is calming and non-aggressive. Green and red are used as indicators. Green confirms that the medication schedule was adhered to and red is intended to be used for warnings, alerts and incomplete fields. It is also used to indicate that a list item (eg: a medication) can be removed from the list.

Typography used

Inter is an open source font designed specifically for digital interfaces. Inter stays highly readable at small sizes. Since this app relies on quickly scanning the interface to identify key info like medication times, Inter’s clean letterforms and number clarity will ensure readability and help prevent mistakes.

Icons

Icons were used in the design to highlight key areas and signify the possibility of user interaction. Several icons were used and sourced from iconbuddy.com

Final Design

Usability Testing

The goal behind a usability test is to evaluate if users can complete the primary functions of the app using the prototype. The test focuses on medication scheduling, logging a dose, adding a new medication and booking an appointment with a doctor. The results will be used to identify usability issues and guide iterative improvements.

A sample of participants (5-10) representing the target audience will complete moderated sessions using a think-aloud approach.They will complete a set of tasks that include signing in, identifying the next medication due, logging a taken dose and adding a new medication with frequency and time etc.

For each task, the moderator will record the outcome, time spent on the task and observable errors (eg: wrong inputs). The participants’ comments will be captured. Additionally, a post-testing survey will be provided to gather feedback.

The findings will be organized into qualitative feedback grouped by areas such as navigation, status understanding and readability. These issues will be prioritized based on severity and addressed in subsequent iterations of the product.

Reflection

Following a structured UX design process helped transform an initial idea based on user pain points into a clearer product that solves specific user problems. It became clear which screen mattered the most to users and what information had to be easily accessible.

The main challenge was balancing simplicity with the complexity of medication management (eg: checking schedule, logging a dose, adding new meds). This was mainly addressed by keeping a consistent structure and ensuring that the pages were not cluttered with information.

Conclusion

MedMinder is a mobile application designed to help users manage their medications and appointments in a simple and reliable way. The solution focuses on reducing the amount of missed doses and forgotten appointments by combining a clear schedule view, an organized medication list and an easy system to add new medications and create reminders. The prototype demonstrates the core experience and the key features required to encourage medication adherence. Future improvements can be made to refine navigation and improve overall accessibility. Overall, the solution is well-positioned to enter the market and be used by users looking to streamline their medication management.
